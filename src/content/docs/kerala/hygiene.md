Many Kerala food customs are warm, communal, and *shared* by design. That sharing is also where germs travel. This page isn't here to scold tradition; it's here to name the genuine risks and offer alternatives that keep the warmth without the downside.

## What's already hygienic

It's worth saying first: a lot of Kerala practice is naturally clean.

- The **banana leaf** is single-use, fresh, and composted, far more hygienic than a wiped-down communal plate.
- **Washing hands before and after** eating is built into the culture of hand-eating.
- Food is usually **cooked fresh** and eaten hot.

## Shared water tumblers

A common sight: one steel tumbler or jug passed around, drunk from by **pouring water into the mouth without touching the lips**. The lip-less pour is a real skill that *reduces* contact, but it doesn't eliminate splash-back, and not everyone manages it cleanly.

:::caution[🧼 Hygiene note]
Provide **individual glasses**, or bottled/poured water per person. If a shared jug is used, keep the no-lip-contact pour and don't let anyone's mouth touch the rim. Wash communal vessels with soap, not just a water rinse.
:::

## Double-dipping and shared dishes

Reaching into a **common bowl of pickle, chutney, or curry with the eating hand**, the same hand that's been to your mouth, transfers saliva and germs to everyone who shares it.

:::caution[🧼 Hygiene note]
Give each diner a **small individual katori** for pickles and chutneys, or use a **dedicated serving spoon** that never touches a mouth or plate. Serve once onto your own plate rather than dipping repeatedly.
:::

## Eating from one large shared platter

Some celebratory traditions, including certain Mappila wedding meals, have **several people eat from a single big platter**. It's a beautiful expression of togetherness, and also a direct route for hand-to-hand germ sharing.

:::caution[🧼 Hygiene note]
Keep the communal platter for its symbolism among people comfortable sharing (close family), and offer **individual leaves or plates** as the default, especially for guests, children, and anyone unwell. No one should feel singled out for choosing their own plate.
:::

## The serving ladle touching every leaf

When a server ladles curry and the spoon **touches each diner's leaf in turn**, it can carry food (and saliva splash) down the entire row.

:::caution[🧼 Hygiene note]
Serve with a **lift-and-pour** so the ladle never contacts the leaf or plate. Use separate spoons per dish.
:::

## Hands, nails, and the basics

:::caution[🧼 Hygiene note]
Wash **both hands with soap** right before eating and again afterwards; a quick rinse isn't enough. Keep **nails trimmed and clean**. Don't blow on hot food at a shared table, and turn away from food to cough or sneeze, then wash up before continuing.
:::

:::note[🧭 Regional variation]
Norms differ between a close family lunch and a meal with guests or in public. The safer practices above matter most when **strangers, children, the elderly, or anyone unwell** share the table.
:::
